Description:

Acetylene burns in air with an intensively hot luminous and smoky flame. Ignition temperature varies according to composition of initial pressure, temperature, and water vapor content. Air mixture containing 30% by volume at atmospheric pressure can be ignited at 581 °F (305 °C).

Uses:
  • Used as a fuel for oxyacetylene cutting, heating-treating, flame cealing to remove rust from steel, spalling concrete and other processes requiring a high temperature flame (3100 °C)
  • Fuel for atomic absorption instruments
  • As raw material for a whole series acid, polyvinylpyrrolidone, chloroprene, neoprene, vinyl acetate, trichloroehtylene and vinyl ethers
Precaution in use:
  • Ventilate to prevent accumulation of Acetylene in flammable concentrations. Use is naturally ventilated areas or if in enclosed area provide forced ventilation.
  • Do not reticulate at pressure greater than 100 kPa
  • Always be mindful that a flammable gas is under pressure.
  • Acetylene can form explosive when mixed with air.
  • No smoking or naked flames within the specified area.
  • Store away from oxidizing sources in a well ventilated area.
  • Close cylinder valve when not i use.
  • Use only approved equipment.
  • Never use Acetylene in direct contact with unalloyed copper, silver and mercury.
  • Must be stored and used in a vertical position.
  • Open cylinder valve slowly.
  • Always use a specified regulator to connect to the system.
  • Do not allow oil or greases on a cylinder or valve.
  • Cylinders should be secured from falling over.
  • Cylinder contains acetone solvent that may cause irritations.
  • Keep the cylinders in an upright position to avoid spillage of solvent inside the cylinder.
  • The maximum hourly draw-off rate from an acetylene cylinder should be limited as follows:
    • Cylinders: 1/7 of nominal contents (i.e for a "G" cylinder content is 7.0 m3 so max draw-off is 1.0 m3 per hour or 16.7 L/min.)
    • Packs: 1/9 of nominal contents (i.e for an MDA-8 pack content is 56 m3, so max draw-off is 6.2 m3 per hour or 278 L/min.

Description:

Argon, making up about 1 percent of the atmosphere, has been steadily increasing in industrial importance and is now being produced to the extent of about 1 billion ft3 annually. Argon can be used interchangeably with helium in many processes. Welding of titanium and other exotic metals in aircraft and rocket construction requires an inert atmosphere, and argon serves this purpose. It is also used in filling incandescent light bulbs, because it does not react with the white-hot tungsten wire as oxygen or nitrogen does. Neon, helium and argon are use in "neon signs".

Uses:
  • Used as a shielding gas (from oxygen) in the welding of metals such as aluminum and stainless steel.
  • Used in refining exotic metals such as zirconium, titanium and many alloys.
  • Mixed with hydrogen for cutting operations and for coating metals with refractory materials.
  • Used extensively in the incandescent lamp industry for the filling of light bulbs.
  • In doping semi-conductors.
  • As gaseous cooling medium in nuclear reactors.
  • To provide a protective atmosphere for growing germanium and silicon crytals for transistors.
  • To provide protective atmosphere in the production of such reactive metals as titanium and zirconium
  • To fill cold weather fluorescent lamps.
  • To trace leaks in refrigeration and other closed system
  • Medical application with oxygen.
  • Used in production of stainless steel by "argon-oxygen decarburizing" process which requires large quantities of argon.
Precaution in use:
  • Asphyxiant in high concentrations.
  • Use only approved pressure rated equipment.
  • Never allow oil or grease on a cylinder or valve.
  • Cylinders should be secured from falling over.
Description:

Carbon dioxide in likewise a componenet of the breath exhaled by animals and humans, because it results from the oxidation of food in the body. Present in the atmosphere to the extent of about 0.03%, its concetration may rise to 1% in a crowed room.

Carbon dioxide is not poisonouse, but too high a concentration in the air (10 to 20 percent) is unhealthy, because it lowers the oxygen concentration and has harmful physiological effets (unconsciousness, failure of certain respiratory muscles, and a change in the pH of the blood).

Uses:
  • Use as a refrigerant and in food preservation as blanketing gas.
  • For pressure spraying and aerosol packaging.
  • Neutralizing agent for pH control and in cement curing.
  • For the transfer of hazardous and flammable liquids.
  • As a diluent with Nitrous Oxide in the manufacture if whipped cream.
  • Inerting agent in fire extinguishrs, in canned food products and for shielded arc welding.
  • Widely used in beverage industry as soda water or carbonated water.
  • Blanketing agent in fire extinguishers.
  • As shield in arc welding
  • Used in chemical industry, such as in making salicylic acid
  • In production of urea.
  • Pressurizing oil well for secondary oil recovery.
  • Specialty uses; chilling aluminum rivets and shrink-fitting machine parts.
Precaution in use:
  • Wear safety glasses, use leather/plastic protective gloves, wear overalls when handling cylinders.
  • Use only approved pressure rated equipment
  • Always use a regulator to connect to system
  • Open cylinder valve slowly.
  • Close cylinder valve when not in use.
  • Lquid carbon dioxide (CO2) should be handled or used only by presons instructed as to the nature of the material.
  • Liquid CO2 equipment should be kept clean.
  • The physical properties of many materials at extreme low temperature may be quite different from the properties of the same materials at normal tempratures.
  • Metals used for liquid CO2 equipment must have satifcafctory physical properties at the low operating temperatures.
  • All pipelines or vessels in which liquid CO2 may be trapped between closed devices. In case where liquid CO2 may be trapped in any valve cavity, venting means should be provided.
  • All safety relief devices should be placed or portected so that water cannot collect or freeze on them in any way, which would interfere with their proper operation.

Description:

Helium, least dense of all elements except hydrogen, is preferred for filling meteorological balloons or dirigibles because, unlike hydrogen it is not combustible. Another interesting use of helium depends upon its very low solubility in water. Because helium is quite insoluble even at elevated pressures, a helium-oxygen mixture instead of compressed air is breathed by diverse and others who work under high pressures. When a person breathes compressed air, considerable nitrogen dissolves in the blood. When he begins to breath air again tiny bubbles in the blood. As these bubbles pass through the capillaries there s extreme pain; this is the condition, suffered by divers and sandhogs, known as the bends.

Uses:
  • Used extensively in the welding industry as an inert shielding gas for arc welding.
  • In mixtures with neon and argon for electronic tubes and neon signs.
  • Used considerably in laboratory and research operations, as a temperature standard, etc.
  • For laboratory equipment as carrier gas such Gas Chromatography, Atomic absorption, etc.
  • A mixture of helium and oxygen finds use as a breathing gas for divers who must work under high pressures and tunnel workers.
  • Filling incandescent flourescent lamps.
  • Coolant for nuclear reactor
  • Refrigerant in cryogenics.
  • Used as protective atmospheres in the fabrication of titanium, zirconium and other metals.
  • Used in the growing transistor crystals.
  • Other uses include lead detection, cryogenic applications, and for generating ultra-low-temperature.
  • In balloons
Precaution in use:
  • Asphyxiant in high concentrations.
  • System must be designed for maximum pressure applicable.
  • Ventilate to preven accumulation of high concentrations of helium and ensure the oxygen level in air is greater than 18%.
  • Wear safety glasses, use leather/plastic gloves, wear overall and safety shoes when handling cylinders.
  • Always use a regulator to connect to system.
Description:

Hydrogen is used in the synthesis of a number of useful compounds such as ammonia and wood alcohol. It is also serves as a reducing agent is the production of tungsten, and as a cooling agent for large generators and transformers (hydrogen is a better conductor of heat than air); it used in the oxy-hydrogen torch and in the atomic hydrogen torch to produce high temperature. Hydrogen was once used to fill balloons and dirigibles, but because of the great fire hazard it has been largely supplanted by helium.

Uses:
  • In the synthesis of ammonia and in petroleum refining operations.
  • Hydrogenation of vegetable and animal oils and fats.
  • Used in metallurgy field because of its ability to reduce metal oxides and prevent oxidation of metals in heat treating certain metals and alloys.
  • Used for balloon inflation
  • Used in semiconductor industry
  • Used in steel shafting manufacturing industry to enhance material hardness.
  • Used with oxygen in oxy-hydrogen welding & cutting, being employed largely in certain brazing operations, welding aluminum and magensium.
  • Cutting metals underwater.
  • For fabrication of quartz and glass.
  • Serves as non-oxidizing shield with other gases.
  • Fuel for powering missiles and rockets
Precaution in use:
  • High flammability (4% to 75%).
  • High pressure leaks can auto ignite.
  • Burns with an almost invincible flame. Suspect leaks should be approached with extreme caution.
  • Store away from oxidizing sources.
  • Use only in well ventilated area.
  • No smoking or naked lights within the specified area.
  • Use only approved equipement which is flameproof.
  • Wear safety glasses, use leather / plastic safety gloves, wear overalls and safety shoes when handling cylinders.
  • Always use a regulator to connect to system.
  • Open cylinder valve slowly. Close cylinder valve when not in use.
Description:

All gases were referred to simply as "air". The few types known were designated by appropriate modifiers such as atmospheric air, dephlogisticated air (oxygen), "inflammable air" (hydrogen), noxious or burnt air (nitrogen), and foul air (gases arising from decaying substances). Oxygen will not burn, but it will support combustion. It is not very active at ordinary temperatures but is active at elevated temperatures.

Uses:
  • In first aid emergencies such as suffocation and heart attacks.
  • In treatment of patients with respiratory disorders.
  • In anesthesia in conjuction with gaseous anesthetics.
  • In hyperbaric oxygenation chambers for treatment of gangrene and carbon monoxide poisoning, and in admixture with helium in asthmatic attacks.
  • Used by firefighters, ambulances and etc.
  • In high-altitude flight.
  • In SCUBA and deep-water diving-aviators breathing oxygen.
  • Metal processing
    • Production of Steel in open-hearth or basic oxygen furnances.
    • Removal of scale from billets by an oxyacetylene flame.
    • Flame cutting and welding.
  • Chemical processes
    • Manufacture of synthetic gas, gasoline, methanol, ammonia, aldehydes, alcohol, acetylene, nitric acid, ethylene oxides, propylene oxides.
    • Synthetic fuels production: coal gasification and coal liquefaction
  • Bioligical process
    • Waste water treatment (aerobic digestion).
Precaution in use:
  • Use only equipment which is approved and degreased for oxygen service.
  • Do not refer to gaseous oxygen as "AIR". Call it by its proper name, "OXYGEN". Do not refer to liquid oxygen as "LOX" or "liquid air". Call it by its proper name, "Liquid Oxygen".
  • Never lubricate oxygen valves, regulators, gages, or fitting with oil or any other combustible substance.
  • Vigorously supports combustion if many materials which will not normally burn in air.
  • Store away from flammable products. Never smoke or carry out work in Oxygen rich atmosphere or clothing saturated with oxygen.
  • Only experienced and properly instructed people should use this product
  • Never permit oil, grease, or other readily combustible substances to come in comtact with oxygen cylinders, valves, regulators, gages and fittings. Oil and oxygen may combine and may exploded when ignited.
  • No smoking or naked light within the specified area.
  • Use only an approved pressure arated equipement and apparatus.
  • Always use a regulator to connect to system.
  • Open cylinder valve slowly.
  • Close cylinder valve when not in use.
  • Cylinders should be secured from falling over.
  • Do not handle oxygen cylinders or apparatus with oily hands or gloves.

Description:

Liquefied Petroleum Gas (LPG) originated from two general sources. Firstly, they are found at the oil or gas fields being removed as condensable products from natural gas and also removed from crude oil during the stabilization process applied in order to reduce the vapor pressure prior to shipments.

Uses:
Being aversatile fuel, LPG may use for the following purpose:
  • DOMESTIC - cooking, water heating, refrigerator, air conditioning.
  • COMMERCIAL - the same uses as in domestic but involving bigger appliances such as in restaurants, hotels and hospital.
  • INDUSTRIAL - flame welding, cutting, soldering, grain drying, tannery, tobacco flue and tobacco curing, and even heating in bakeries.
  • Other uses include incubation purposes (Poultry Farms), in motor vehicles and tractors.
Precaution in use:
Safety Handling of LPG:

To ensure safety, it is equally important that persons engaged in handling of LPG understand its properties. They should be thoroughly trained in safety procedures for handling distribution and operation.

  • Full and empty cylinders must be loaded and transported on trucks in an upright position. They should be secured or chained to prevent shifting or falling.
  • Always close cylinder valves before disconnection it from a hose line or manifold.
  • The cylinder valve connection should be examined to determine the conditions of the threads. Those with worn or burned threads should be replaced. Dirt on either the connection or the cylinder valve should be removed.
  • Each time the cylinders are changed, the delivery man should make a safety check on related equipment, i.e. pigtails, regulators, and change-over valves. If there are piles of rubbish or other hazards near the installation, it must be removed.
  • Cylinders should be set plumb and square. Use hollow blocks and concrete slabs as a base and when hood are used, proper closure should be made.
  • After the full tanks have been set and connection made, cylinders should be opened and connections checked for leaks. In checking leaks, use the soap test.
  • Plastic caps should always be placed on the cylinders to prevent foreign matters such as dirt, water, etc. from entering the cylinder valves.
  • In case of a relief valve cylinder opens while transported or set, the cylinder should be moved to a safe place and vented until the pressure is lowered.
